Live Consulting Client: ACHP (Alliance of Community Health Plans) • 2025 - Ongoing

CMS Medicare Advantage market analysis shaping national healthcare advocacy

A live analytics engagement for the Alliance of Community Health Plans, a national nonprofit representing nonprofit and community-based health plans that advocates on Medicare Advantage policy in Washington. The analysis produces evidence that informs ACHP's policy positions on benefit design, market structure, and congressional advocacy - work that can influence how Medicare Advantage is regulated and therefore how millions of seniors receive care.

  • Built a four-step ingestion pipeline across CMS Landscape, ACHP Crosswalk, monthly CPSC enrollment, and plan benefit (PBP) data, producing dual-year 2025 and 2026 master files of 138K+ plans across 208 columns.
  • Ran a supplemental benefit reduction analysis across dental, vision, hearing, transportation, OTC, and fitness benefits, identifying year-over-year benefit retention differences between ACHP member plans and the broader market.
  • Validated k-means segmentation using three independent criteria - elbow curve, silhouette score, and Davies-Bouldin index - then ran forced k=4 and k=5 solutions to stress-test cluster stability.
  • Extended segmentation with region-level clustering and stratified HMO-vs-PPO clustering to surface structural differences masked by a single national model.
  • Produced three findings used in ACHP's internal strategy discussions, including one that shifted advocacy emphasis toward transportation and hearing benefit retention - areas where nonprofit plans differentiate from national for-profit carriers.

Healthcare AI Live Build • 2026

RxCheck, a pharmacist-facing interaction tracker with a disciplined LLM boundary

A clinical decision support prototype where the interesting engineering is not the AI, it is the data model and the boundary drawn around what the AI is allowed to do. Deterministic interaction checks run against a locally-imported clinical database; an LLM layer produces plain-English explanations under strict RAG with schema-validated output. Designed with FDA Non-Device CDS criteria in mind so a pharmacist can always see the basis of every alert.

  • Two-table interaction model with a canonical pair row and per-source assertions, lexicographic DDI ordering enforced by CHECK constraint to prevent (A,B)/(B,A) duplicates at the database level.
  • RxNorm normalization pipeline with exact match -> brand lookup -> trigram fuzzy match -> NDC fallback, with confirmed fuzzy matches written back as aliases so repeat typos resolve instantly.
  • RAG-only LLM layer: structured interaction rows injected as context, schema validation on every response, cross-check that the model only references drugs present in the source data. Failed validations are logged, never shown.
  • Hub drug scoring surfaces which single medication change would reduce interaction burden most for polypharmacy patients - a question pure severity ranking cannot answer.
  • HIPAA-ready schema shape with patient identifiers in a 1:1 child table so column-level encryption or physical separation can be added without touching any other table.
  • Frozen run snapshots mean the audit trail reflects what the pharmacist actually saw, not what the data says now.

Why this matters

A warning system that cries wolf is worse than one that is silent. The design discipline here - tiered severity, per-patient acknowledgments, severity-escalation detection, gated DDSI on recorded conditions - exists because alert fatigue is the failure mode that actually matters in a pharmacy workflow.

What was hard

The hardest problem wasn't model routing or streaming - it was inventory deduplication. When ingredients came from multiple sources (fridge scans, manual input, receipts), the system produced duplicates like "chicken breast," "chicken," and "raw chicken" as separate items. I solved this with a lightweight fuzzy matching approach using bidirectional substring containment, collapsing variations into a single entry without heavy model overhead. It's not perfect - edge cases still exist, and a production system would likely use embeddings or richer training data - but it was the most unexpectedly interesting challenge, sitting at the intersection of data consistency, UX, and applied AI.
